## Voice Signals from the FY26 Message House (Simpro Group Tab)

The latest Group-level positioning (sheet `1VVfWFFohv5IyroehwuRrJGLyvyyXv0Z5JjiU_elIrbs`, tab `Simpro Group`, gid 497641548) is the **most current source** for voice and tone. These are the explicit signals it provides — treat as authoritative until the canonical Voice Style Guide doc is accessible.

### Four-word tone of voice
**Authoritative. Bold. Confident. Visionary.**

This is a deliberate compression of the older 6-word descriptor set (Inspirational, Challenging, Approachable, Confident, Clear & Direct, Clever). The newer four are sharper and should be the standard for Simpro brand voice going forward.

- **Authoritative**: We have the right to lead — 24,000+ businesses, 450,000+ users, 20+ years. Speak from operating knowledge, not theory.
- **Bold**: Take a position. "Double trades business profitability" is a claim with stakes. We don't hedge.
- **Confident**: Conviction without arrogance. We back claims with named customers and named numbers.
- **Visionary**: We're not selling software; we're building "a future where the trades are respected, recognized, and fully rewarded for the work that powers the world." Frame product capabilities against that horizon.

### The brand pillars (Simpro Group, FY26): Profits · AI-first · Productivity · Trades Champion

These are the four words that organize every piece of marketing. Use these exact terms:

- **Profits** (not "Profitability" — shorter, more direct, more visceral). The Simpro brand-specific tab uses "Profitability" as the pillar name, which is acceptable for product-page contexts. In top-of-funnel brand voice, prefer "Profits."
- **AI-first** (hyphenated, lowercase "first"). Never "AI-powered," "AI-enabled," or "powered by AI" — those frame AI as an additive. "AI-first" is the differentiator and must be preserved.
- **Productivity** (not "Operational Control" or "Operational Efficiency"). The Group framing favors "Productivity" because it lands with frontline trades operators; the older Simpro tab "Operational Control" reads as enterprise-speak.
- **Trades Champion** (capital T, capital C — always proper-noun when used as a pillar name). Lowercased only when used as a descriptive phrase: "we champion the trades."

### Mission, vision, and category (verbatim — do not paraphrase)

**Mission**: *Double trades and field service business profitability.*
- 8 words. Use the full phrase in About / hero / press contexts. Don't truncate to "double profitability" — the audience qualifier (*trades and field service business*) is part of the claim.

**Vision**: *Build a future where the trades are respected, recognized, and fully rewarded for the work that powers the world.*
- Use verbatim in About-style copy and culture content. In body copy, the shorter phrase "the work that powers the world" can stand in.

**Category line**: *AI-first operating platform for the trades.*
- This is the category-defining phrase. Use it consistently in meta descriptions, hero subheadings, and AI-citation-bait sentences. Variations like "AI-first field service platform" are acceptable when the SEO target requires it; "AI-first FSM software" is not.

**Brand Promise**: *We empower trade and field service businesses with AI-first tools that make your work more profitable, your days more predictable, and your business stronger.*
- Three-part promise rhythm: **more profitable / more predictable / stronger**. The pattern (comparative-adj, comparative-adj, comparative-adj) is reusable for hero copy elsewhere.

### Group Values (use sparingly in marketing — they are internal-culture-first)
- We are one team
- We are accountable
- We are customer centric
- We are growth minded
- We celebrate success

Surface these on Careers pages, employer-brand content, and culture stories. Avoid in product/feature copy unless directly relevant.

### Audience phrasing (verbatim — preserve in hero and conversion copy)

**Simpro brand**: *Trade and field service business owners and decision-makers who want one platform to manage their business, increase profitability, streamline operations, elevate employee and customer experiences, and drive sustainable long-term growth.*

Key voice signals:
- "Trade and field service business owners and decision-makers" — NOT "SMBs," "small businesses," or "operators" (those are casual shorthand for internal use)
- "One platform" — single platform is a load-bearing claim against point solutions
- Five outcomes in parallel structure: manage / increase profitability / streamline / elevate / drive — preserve the rhythm in audience-facing copy
- "Sustainable long-term growth" — not "rapid growth" or "hypergrowth" (mismatched for trades audience)

### Boilerplate patterns (rhetorical structure to reuse)

The 25/50/100-word boilerplates from the Simpro Group tab share a recurring structure that should inform headline and intro writing:

1. **Claim the category**: "Simpro Group builds the AI operating platform for the trades..."
2. **State the outcome**: "...helping contractors, technicians, and field crews get paid faster, run smarter, and grow stronger."
3. **Prove the scale**: "...24,000 businesses and 450,000 users worldwide."
4. **Geo-credibility**: "...with teams across the U.S., Australia, and the U.K."
5. **Champion close**: "...we champion the people who keep the world running."
6. **CTA + canonical domain**: "Learn more at simpro.ai."

This six-beat structure is a usable template for hero sections, About pages, and PR boilerplate. Triplet rhythms — *get paid faster, run smarter, and grow stronger* — are signature. Use them.

### Canonical domain: simpro.ai (not simprogroup.com)

The FY26 boilerplate references **simpro.ai** as the canonical destination. Older content and the FY25 200-word boilerplate referenced simprogroup.com. **Default to simpro.ai for new content** unless a specific page only exists on simprogroup.com.

### Signature phrasing — preserve verbatim where used

These short phrases appear repeatedly across the FY26 Message House and should be treated as proprietary brand language:

- *AI-first operating platform for the trades*
- *Double trades business profitability*
- *The people who keep the world running*
- *The work that powers the world*
- *Get paid faster, run smarter, and grow stronger*
- *Built for the trades* (not "for SMBs" or "for service businesses")
- *Trades Champion* (capitalized when standing alone as a pillar; lowercase "champion the trades" as a verb phrase)
- *Make your work more profitable, your days more predictable, and your business stronger*
- *Champion the people who keep the world running*

When in doubt: if the phrase is in the Message House, prefer the verbatim wording over a paraphrase.

### Verbs that fit Simpro voice

The Message House and battlecards consistently use a tight set of action verbs. Prefer these in headlines and CTAs:

**Outcome verbs**: simplify, transform, eliminate (complexity / paperwork / delays), turn (chaos into profit, complexity into clarity, data into decisions), unlock, double, accelerate, scale, grow, empower, champion, reward.

**Operational verbs**: quote, schedule, dispatch, invoice, collect, track, forecast, automate, surface, integrate, run, manage.

**Verbs to avoid**: leverage, enable, optimize (overused), facilitate, utilize, deliver (as in "deliver value"), drive (as in "drive efficiencies" — fine in "drive growth"), maximize. These read corporate, not trades.

### Sentence rhythm

Two patterns dominate the Message House copy and should be the default:

1. **The two-beat punch**: Short. Then expanded. "Real-time job costing. Real-time profitability." / "Double Your Profits, Not Your Hours." / "From chaos to clarity — run your business on one platform."
2. **The triplet**: Three parallel constructions. "Get paid faster, run smarter, and grow stronger." / "More profitable, more predictable, stronger." / "Quote. Schedule. Track. Monitor. Report. Get Paid."

Avoid four-or-more parallel constructions unless they're functional (a feature list). They lose rhythm.

### What the Message House signals about what to AVOID

The Message House contains zero instances of:
- "Cutting-edge," "world-class," "innovative" (as adjectives) — vendor cliches
- "Solutions" as a generic noun stand-in for product
- "Best-in-class" (despite being an internal favorite for boilerplate; it appears in the older 25 - Simpro tab but disappears from the newer Simpro Group tab — a deliberate trim)
- "Leverage" / "facilitate" / "utilize" — corporate verbs
- "Hardworking SMBs" or paternalistic descriptors for the audience

The audience descriptor is always *trades*, *trade and field service business owners and decision-makers*, *contractors, technicians, and field crews*. Use these.

## Grammar & Mechanics

### Capitalization

**Headlines & H1 Page Titles**: Title Case for hero/page-level headlines (e.g., "Field Service Management Software for the Trades"). Sentence case for blog post titles is also acceptable — match the template the section is being written for.

**H2 / H3 Subheadings**: Sentence case ("How real-time job costing changes margin visibility"). Consistent across an article — don't mix.

**Product Names**:
- **Simpro** — always capitalized, never "simpro" or "SIMPRO"
- **Simpro Group** — proper noun, both words capitalized
- **AroFlo**, **BigChange**, **ClockShark** — internal capitalization preserved (AroFlo is camel-case, not "Aroflo")
- **Simpro Payments**, **Simpro API**, **Multi-Company** — capitalized as feature names
- **Digital Forms**, **Data Feed**, **Maintenance Planner**, **Takeoffs**, **Delight** — capitalized as product/add-on names

**Industry Terms**:
- *trades*, *trade businesses*, *trades and field service businesses* — lowercase
- *field service management* — lowercase; abbreviation **FSM** introduced on first acronym use
- *job management* — lowercase
- *job costing*, *quoting*, *invoicing*, *dispatch* — lowercase nouns/verbs

### Numbers

**When to Spell Out**:
- Spell out: one through nine
- Use numerals: 10 and above
- Exceptions (always numerals):
  - Percentages: 5%, 60%
  - Money: $5, $36B, $100M
  - Measurements / counts paired with units: 250K users, 8.5K customers, 21M quotes
  - Lists/stats: always numerals for scannability

**Large Numbers**:
- Use abbreviated forms in marketing copy: 250K+, 8.5K+, 14M+, $36B+
- Spell out in formal/legal contexts: 250,000+ users, $36 billion+
- Be consistent within a section — don't mix "250K" and "250,000" in the same paragraph

### Punctuation

**Oxford Comma**: **Yes** — use the Oxford comma ("scheduling, dispatch, and inventory tracking"). Consistent with US English convention used in the FY26 Message House.

**Em Dashes**: Use em dash (—) with no spaces for parenthetical emphasis ("real-time job costing—the difference between knowing and guessing"). Avoid hyphens with spaces (-) as a substitute.

**Quotation Marks**: Use curly/smart quotes in published content ("Work. Smarter."). Straight quotes are acceptable in draft Markdown and convert at publish time.

**Periods on Bullets**:
- Complete sentence bullets: end with a period
- Fragment bullets: no period
- Be consistent within a list — all fragments or all sentences, not mixed

### Abbreviations & Acronyms

**First Use**:
- Spell out on first use, acronym in parentheses: "field service management (FSM)"
- Universally known acronyms: SEO, URL, CTA, AI, API, CRM, ERP, SaaS, SSO, KPI, ROI, B2B — don't expand
- Trades-specific known acronyms: HVAC, A/V, PM (preventative maintenance), PO (purchase order), MRR — usually safe to use without expansion when context is clear

**Common Trades / Industry Acronyms**:
- FSM: Field Service Management
- PM: Preventative Maintenance (also Project Manager — disambiguate by context)
- TCO: Total Cost of Ownership
- BI: Business Intelligence
- ICP: Ideal Customer Profile
- BDR: Business Development Representative
- SE: Sales Engineer
- CSM: Customer Success Manager
- MRR / ARR: Monthly / Annual Recurring Revenue
- A/V: Audio / Visual (in low voltage industry context)

**Latin Abbreviations**:
- *e.g.*, *i.e.*, *etc.* — use sparingly; prefer plain English ("for example," "in other words," "and so on")
- When used, italicize and follow with a comma: *e.g.,*

## Word Choice & Usage

### Preferred Terms

**Say This** → **Not That** (with reason):

- *trades and field service businesses* → *SMBs* / *small businesses* — audience identifies by industry, not size
- *trades professionals* / *trades operators* / *contractors* → *blue-collar workers* — condescending
- *trades* → *the trades industry* — redundant
- *field technicians* / *techs* → *workers* / *laborers* — wrong register
- *AI-first* → *AI-powered* — Simpro leads with AI, doesn't bolt it on
- *job costing* → *expense tracking* — industry-specific term, don't substitute
- *recurring maintenance* / *PM* → *follow-up jobs* — incorrect terminology
- *quote* (noun) → *estimate* — Simpro and the wider category use "quote" as the primary term; "estimate" appears in feature names but "quote" is preferred in body copy
- *dispatch* → *send out* — keep the industry verb
- *get paid faster* → *improve cash conversion* — too corporate
- *Simpro* → *the Simpro platform* / *the Simpro solution* — drop the article in second references and beyond
- *AI Mobile Work Notes* / *Delight* / *Digital Forms* — use proper product names, not paraphrases

### Words to Avoid
- *very*, *really*, *actually* — almost always cuttable
- *robust*, *seamless*, *powerful*, *world-class*, *cutting-edge*, *game-changing*, *revolutionary* — vendor cliches
- *solutions* as a generic noun for "product" — too vague
- *unlock*, *empower*, *leverage* (overused; use sparingly and only when no better verb exists)
- *click here*, *read more*, *learn more* (when used as standalone link text) — descriptive anchors only
- *In today's [competitive/digital/fast-paced] world* — dead opening
- *passive constructions when active is clearer* — "Simpro automates invoicing" not "Invoicing is automated by Simpro"

### Inclusive Language
- Use **they** as the default singular pronoun ("a trades operator should be able to ... they ...")
- Avoid gendered defaults ("his crew," "tradesman" alone) — prefer *trades professional*, *technician*, *crew member*
- Avoid idioms that don't translate across US/AU/NZ/UK English ("ballpark figure," "homerun") in international content
- Be specific about audience: *trades businesses*, *commercial contractors*, *residential operators* — not "everyone in the trades"

### Regional Spelling
- **US English is the default** for simpro.com / simpro.ai (per FY26 Message House)
- Regional sites (AU, NZ, UK) should localize: *optimise* (UK/AU) vs. *optimize* (US); *centralised* vs. *centralized*; *colour* vs. *color*
- Note: The Group Message House uses British spelling in some pillar copy ("centralised") because Simpro Group has AU/UK heritage. The US-facing content should use US spellings consistently.

## Formatting Standards

### Text Formatting

**Bold**:
- Use for: key concepts, important takeaways, scannable emphasis, product/feature names on first major mention
- Don't overuse: if every paragraph has a bold phrase, nothing stands out
- Example: "**Real-time job costing** is the difference between knowing and guessing."

**Italics**:
- Use for: emphasis (sparingly), titles of works, foreign or technical terms on first mention
- Avoid italics for whole sentences or block quotes — use blockquote or callout instead

**Underline**: Avoid (reserved for links).

**ALL CAPS**: Avoid except for standard acronyms (FSM, SEO, AI, SaaS, etc.).

### Lists

**Bulleted Lists**:
- Use for non-sequential items, features, benefits
- Capitalize the first word of each bullet
- Parallel structure: all bullets the same part of speech (all verbs, all nouns, all sentences, or all fragments)
- Period only on complete-sentence bullets
- 3–7 bullets is the sweet spot; more than 9 splits into sub-sections

**Numbered Lists**:
- Use for sequential steps, rankings, ordered priorities
- Format: "1." "2." "3." (with period, no parentheses)
- Same capitalization/punctuation rules as bulleted lists

**Nested Lists**:
- Maximum 2 levels deep for readability
- Use different markers (hyphen → bullet) for sub-items in Markdown

### Links

**Anchor Text**:
- Descriptive — tell the reader where they're going
- Keyword-rich when natural; never stuffed
- 2–5 words typically
- ✅ "Learn how Simpro Payments works" / "See real-time job costing in action"
- ❌ "Click here" / "Read more" / "This page"

**Internal vs. External**:
- Internal links: open in same tab
- External links: open in new tab (`target="_blank"`)
- All external links: `rel="noopener noreferrer"` for security

### Code & Technical Elements

**Inline Code**:
- Use backticks for URLs, file names, API endpoints, code snippets
- Example: "Send a POST to `/api/jobs` to create a job programmatically."

**Code Blocks**:
- Use fenced code blocks with language identifier (` ```python `, ` ```json `, etc.)
- Comment within code where the why isn't obvious

### Callouts / Asides

- Use blockquote (`>`) for customer quotes and pull quotes
- Use bolded "**Note:**", "**Tip:**", "**Important:**" lead-ins for inline asides
- Reserve full callout boxes for genuinely critical context (warnings, caveats, key takeaways)

## Content Structure

### Article Introduction
**Standard Structure** (150–250 words):
1. **Hook** (1–2 sentences): A specific question, statistic, or contrarian statement. Avoid "In today's competitive market..."
2. **Problem** (2–3 sentences): The challenge a trades operator faces around this topic.
3. **Promise** (2–3 sentences): What the reader will learn or be able to do.
4. **Credibility** (optional, 1–2 sentences): Quick proof of authority (e.g., "Drawing on Simpro's work with 250,000+ users across 8,500 trades businesses...")

**Keyword Placement**:
- Primary keyword must appear in the first 100 words
- Natural integration only — never force

### Section Length
- **Minimum**: 150 words per H2 section
- **Maximum**: 500 words per H2 (split into H3 subsections if longer)
- **Ideal**: 250–350 words per main section

### Conclusion
**Standard Structure** (150–250 words):
1. **Recap** (3–5 bullets or short paragraph): Key takeaways
2. **Action** (1–2 sentences): What the reader should do next
3. **CTA** (1–2 sentences): Specific Simpro CTA matched to funnel stage
4. **Forward-looking** (optional, 1 sentence): The bigger possibility this unlocks

## SEO-Specific Style

### Meta Titles
- 50–60 characters including spaces
- Include primary keyword
- Brand suffix: " | Simpro" when space allows (saves room over " - Simpro")
- No ending punctuation

### Meta Descriptions
- 150–160 characters including spaces
- Include primary keyword
- Include a verb-driven CTA ("See how," "Learn why," "Discover")
- End with a complete thought, not mid-sentence

### URL Slugs
- Lowercase only
- Hyphens between words
- Include primary keyword
- 3–5 words ideal
- Format: `/blog/primary-keyword-phrase` (or follow the existing Simpro URL pattern when extending category content)

### Alt Text
- Describe what the image shows
- Include relevant keyword naturally when it fits
- 125 characters or less
- No "image of" or "picture of" prefixes (implied by being alt text)

## Dates & Time

**Date Format**:
- US-facing content: Month DD, YYYY (e.g., "January 15, 2026")
- International / formal contexts: DD Month YYYY (e.g., "15 January 2026")
- Numeric dates: avoid ambiguous formats (1/3/26 reads differently in US vs. AU). Use ISO 2026-01-03 for technical/internal content.

**Time**:
- 12-hour format with a.m. / p.m.: "3:00 p.m. ET"
- Always specify time zone when scheduling is relevant ("3:00 p.m. AEST")

## Statistics & Data

### Citing Sources
- Always cite statistics with their source
- Format: "According to [Source], [statistic]." with link to source on first mention
- Include year of data: "In 2025, the average trades business..."
- For Simpro-internal stats, attribute clearly: "Across Simpro's 8,500+ customers..."

### Presenting Numbers
- Round large numbers for scannability: "$36B+ in invoices/year" beats "$36,000,000,000+"
- Use % symbol: 60% (not "60 percent")
- Use $ for money: $750K (not "750 thousand dollars")
- Use commas as thousands separator: 250,000 (not 250000)
- Be precise with named-customer outcomes: "Shaffer Beacon Mechanical: 60% increase in profit margin" — don't round the customer-specific number to "approximately 60%"

## Images & Media

### Image Captions
- **Optional** — use only when adding context the image itself doesn't convey
- Sentence case, end with a period if complete sentence
- Placement: below the image

### Screenshots
- Crop tightly to the relevant area
- Add arrows / highlights / callouts if needed to draw attention to a specific element
- Update screenshots when product UI changes — outdated UI screenshots damage trust
- Alt text describes what the screenshot shows, including the feature name

### Charts & Graphs
- Use sparingly — only when data visualization improves understanding
- Keep design clean: minimal gridlines, clear labels, brand colors
- Always provide context in the surrounding text
- Alt text describes the key finding, not just "chart"

## Brand-Specific Guidelines

### Simpro Product References

**The Simpro Platform**:
- Refer to it as "Simpro" (first reference) and "the platform" (subsequent references)
- Avoid "the Simpro platform" repeatedly — use "Simpro" or "the platform"
- Avoid "Simpro software" — the platform is the product

**Add-Ons**:
- Lead with the add-on's value, not just its name: "Simpro Digital Forms turns paper recordkeeping into searchable digital records."
- Add-on names are proper nouns: **Digital Forms**, **Data Feed**, **Maintenance Planner**, **Takeoffs**, **Multi-Company**, **Delight**

**AI Features**:
- Refer to specific in-product AI capabilities by name: **AI Mobile Work Notes**, **AI-assisted data entry**, **smart scheduling**, **Delight** (AI customer marketing agent)
- "AI" alone is acceptable; "Simpro AI" works as a category descriptor; avoid "artificial intelligence" in body copy (use only on technical pages)

### Competitor References

Approach: **Name competitors directly when relevant, focus on differentiation, never disparage.**

- Name them: BuildOps, Housecall Pro, ServiceTrade, ServiceTitan, FieldEdge, FieldPulse, etc.
- Frame around customer outcomes ("Foster Plumbing chose Simpro for ...") not vendor criticisms ("BuildOps is bad at ...")
- Use the battlecard "Quick Competitive Plays" language as a model — it's pressure-tested and fair
- Cite the customer story behind a competitive claim wherever possible (Shaffer Beacon, Hilliers Refrigeration, etc.)

## Accessibility

### Screen Reader Friendly
- Descriptive link text (not "click here")
- Image alt text for all meaningful images (decorative images get `alt=""`)
- Proper heading hierarchy: H1 → H2 → H3, never skip levels
- Descriptive button labels ("Book a demo" not "Submit")

### Plain Language
- Write at 8th–10th grade level (trades audience values clarity over corporate sophistication)
- Define technical terms on first use unless context is clear
- Use short sentences (15–22 words avg)
- Break complex ideas into digestible chunks

## Editing Checklist

- [ ] Spelling checked (US English for simpro.com / simpro.ai; regional for AU/NZ/UK sites)

- [ ] Strong intro (hook, problem, promise)
