## Tone of Voice

### Canonical descriptors (Voice Style Guide, August 2024)

**Confident. Clear. Clever. Trustworthy. Inspirational. Direct.**

Speak like an innovative tech company with proven field service history. Messages are direct, aspirational, and tailored to audience goals and technical level. See `style-guide.md` for the full **voice is / voice is not** table and channel-specific tone shifts (social, email, blogs, video).

### FY26 hero messaging overlay (Message House)

**Authoritative. Bold. Confident. Visionary.**

Use these four words for **brand/hero/pillar** copy and FY26 campaigns. They sharpen the August 2024 set for AI-first positioning. See `style-guide.md` → "Voice Signals from the FY26 Message House" for definitions. Do not drop the August 2024 "clever," "trustworthy," or "inspirational" signals in long-form or educational content.

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Authoritative
- **What it means**: We have earned the right to lead this category. 20+ years in field service, 24,000+ businesses, 450,000+ users, and $36B+ invoices processed annually. We speak from real operating knowledge — not theory.
- **How it sounds**: Direct. Definitive. We don't hedge or qualify when we know the answer.
- **Example**: "Simpro runs service, recurring maintenance, and projects in a single system — with native asset management, PM, and compliance."
- **Avoid**: "We hope," "we try," "we might be able to." Soft language that signals uncertainty.

### 2. Ambitious
- **What it means**: We push trades businesses to think bigger about what their business can be. Growth-minded isn't a slogan — it's the lens we run every conversation through.
- **How it sounds**: Forward-leaning. Framed around what's possible, not what's broken.
- **Example**: "Double Your Profits, Not Your Hours."
- **Avoid**: Status-quo language. Lists of features that don't ladder up to a bigger win.

### 3. Trades-Focused
- **What it means**: We talk to plumbers, electricians, fire & security contractors, HVAC operators, A/V installers — not "SMBs" or "users." We know their day, their stack, their margins, their pain.
- **How it sounds**: Industry-fluent. We use the trades' vocabulary (job costing, recurring maintenance, PM, takeoffs, dispatch) without explaining the terms in-line.
- **Example**: "Every Job, a Profit Engine."
- **Avoid**: Generic SaaS speak. Treating field service as an abstract category. "Solutioning."

### 4. Practical
- **What it means**: We sell outcomes, not features. We respect the reader's time and the fact that they're probably reading this on a phone between two jobs.
- **How it sounds**: Plain language. Concrete proof points. Short sentences.
- **Example**: "We get you live in 5–8 weeks with hands-on onboarding."
- **Avoid**: Buzzwords, layered jargon, paragraph-long hero copy.

### 5. Outcomes-Driven
- **What it means**: Every claim ties back to a number — margin, hours, revenue, time-to-paid, customers retained. If we can't quantify the win, we earn the right to make the claim with a customer story.
- **How it sounds**: Specific. Named customers, named numbers. "60% increase in profit margin (Shaffer Beacon Mechanical)" beats "significant profit improvements."
- **Example**: "Foster Plumbing grew 10x in six years, improved margin by 10%, cut collection times by 33%, and sold at a 6x EBITDA multiple."
- **Avoid**: Vague impact claims. "Significantly faster," "much more efficient," "world-class."

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone
Imagine you're a seasoned trades operator who got the tech right and now helps peers do the same. You know the work — quoting, scheduling, invoicing, getting paid, recurring maintenance, asset compliance. You're confident without being preachy. You respect the reader's intelligence and time. You celebrate the trades as the industry that keeps the world running and refuse to treat them as a back-office afterthought.

**Canonical Voice Style Guide**: Confident, clear, clever, trustworthy, inspirational, direct.

**FY26 Simpro hero overlay**: Authoritative, bold, confident, visionary.

**Operational writing traits** (from Message House + style guide): Trades-focused, practical, outcomes-driven, growth-minded, empathetic, product-proud (not salesy).

### Tone Variations by Content Type

**How-To Guides**: Instructive, step-by-step, encouraging (blog tone).
- "Here's the fastest path from quote to invoice."
- "Run this report on Monday — it'll show you which jobs are bleeding margin."
- "Most teams stall at this step. Here's how to clear it in under 10 minutes."

**Strategy/Advice Content**: Authoritative, experienced, decisive.
- "If you're growing past 20 techs, this is the workflow that breaks first."
- "The trades businesses that double profitability all do these three things."
- "Stop running service and projects on the same workflow. Here's why."

**Industry News/Trends**: Insightful, AI-aware, plain-spoken.
- "AI in the trades isn't about chatbots. It's about smaller invoices missed and bigger margins earned."
- "Here's what changed in the field service category this quarter and what it means for contractors."

**Product/Feature Content**: Benefit-focused, named-outcome, proof-led.
- "AI Mobile Work Notes reduces typing after site visits so techs can get job notes back to the office faster."
- "Real-time job costing means you know whether a job made money before the truck leaves the site."
- "Multi-Company lets one Simpro instance run multiple business units with roll-up reporting."

### Message Map Narrative Models

Use these as narrative inputs for Simpro Group, Simpro, and Lightning-adjacent content when they fit the brief. For Lightning-specific copy, apply `context/lightning-positioning.md` first.

- **Execution capacity**: The gap between average and best-in-class profitability is not effort; it is the operating capacity to do the work the business cannot afford to staff.
- **The two jobs**: A trade business has the physical job in the field and the business job behind it: quoting, invoicing, payroll, purchasing, compliance, reporting, and customer communication.
- **Chaos to control**: The opposite of operational chaos is control, not calm. Frame control as shared visibility, connected data, and workflows that keep the business from relying on one person's memory.
- **Connected data**: AI claims are strongest when tied to operational data that flows across scheduling, field work, materials, quoting, invoicing, payments, and reporting.

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: Mix short, declarative sentences with longer explanatory ones. Open and close paragraphs with the shortest sentence.
- **Active voice preferred**: "Simpro automates invoicing" — not "Invoicing is automated by Simpro."
- **Average length**: 15–22 words per sentence.
- **Clarity first**: If a sentence needs to be re-read, it's wrong. Rewrite.

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: 2–4 sentences typically.
- **One idea per paragraph**: One point, one paragraph. No piling up.
- **Transitions**: Use the previous paragraph's last word/idea as the bridge. Avoid "However," "Furthermore," "Additionally" as crutches.
- **White space**: Generous. Mobile reads first.

### Word Choice
- **Trades-fluent**: Use the vocabulary the audience uses — job costing, takeoffs, dispatch, PM, recurring maintenance, asset register, compliance log.
- **Specific over vague**: "$36B in invoices per year" not "huge volumes." "60% margin increase at Shaffer Beacon Mechanical" not "significant profit improvements."
- **Active verbs**: Quote, schedule, dispatch, invoice, collect, forecast, scale, automate, surface, ladder.
- **Avoid**: "Solutions" as a generic stand-in for product. "Game-changing." "Robust." "Seamless" (overused). "World-class." "Powerful" without a number behind it. **All-in-one** (say *centralized* or *end-to-end*). Calling buyers **tradies**. **24/7 live support** (Simpro offers **24/6 live support**).

### Terminology

**Say This** → **Not That**
- *trades and field service businesses* → *SMBs* / *small businesses* (the audience identifies by industry, not size)
- *trades professionals* / *trades operators* → *blue-collar workers* (condescending)
- *field service management* → *FSM* (don't open with acronyms)
- *field technicians* / *techs* → *workers* / *laborers*
- *AI-first* → *AI-powered* (we lead with AI, not bolt it on)
- *job costing* → *expense tracking* (industry has a specific term)
- *recurring maintenance* / *PM* → *follow-up jobs*
- *Simpro* → *the Simpro platform* / *the platform* (use the brand name; secondary references can drop "the platform")
- *license* → *package* / *plan* / *subscription* / *contract*
- *trade and field service leaders* → *tradies* (except narrow SEO/demand-gen)
- *centralized* / *end-to-end* → *all-in-one*

## Content Formatting

### Headlines
- **Be specific**: "Double Your Profits, Not Your Hours" beats "Boost Your Business."
- **Lead with the customer's win, not the product**: "Every Job, a Profit Engine" — not "Simpro Tracks Job Profitability."
- **Use rhythm**: Pairs and lists of three land harder than single ideas. "Quote. Schedule. Track. Monitor. Report. Get Paid."
- **Keep it under 60 characters for SEO**, under 9 words for hero copy.

### Subheadings
- **Sentence case** for body subheads (H2/H3). Title Case acceptable for page-level H1 hero copy.
- **Promise + payoff** structure: state what the section delivers in the heading.
- **Skim-test**: A reader who only reads the subheads should still get the argument.

### Lists
- **Parallel structure**: All bullets start with the same part of speech (all verbs, all nouns, all complete sentences).
- **Front-load the win**: Lead with the outcome, follow with the mechanism. "Get paid faster — integrated invoicing and payments."
- **Three to five bullets is the sweet spot**. More than seven and the list loses force.
- **No periods on fragment bullets**. Periods only on complete-sentence bullets.

### Calls-to-Action
- **Action verb + outcome**: "See Simpro in action," "Book a demo," "Calculate your ROI."
- **Specific over generic**: Avoid "Learn more" / "Click here."
- **Match the funnel stage**: Top-of-funnel = read/watch. Mid = compare/calculate. Bottom = book a demo / start a trial.

## Voice Examples

### Excellent Simpro Voice ✅

"Foster Plumbing grew 10x in six years, with Simpro as the operational backbone. They improved margin by 10%, cut collection times by 33%, and sold at a 6x EBITDA multiple. 'Simpro helped us achieve real-time job costing, build a $10M plumbing business, and played a huge role in our successful exit.' — Amy Carnrick, Former CEO, Foster Plumbing."

**Why this works**:
- Specific, quantified outcomes — not abstract claims
- Names the customer, the trade, and the exit metric
- The quote does the selling; we just frame it
- Punchy, no buzzwords, no hedging
- Mirrors how trades leaders actually talk about wins

### Not Simpro Voice ❌

"Our innovative, cloud-native field service management solution leverages cutting-edge AI capabilities to deliver world-class outcomes for businesses of all sizes, empowering you to unlock new efficiencies and revolutionize the way you operate in today's dynamic marketplace."

**Why this fails**:
- Generic SaaS language — could be any vendor
- Buzzword soup: "innovative," "cloud-native," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "unlock," "revolutionize"
- Zero numbers, zero customers, zero specifics
- Reads like it was written by committee
- Doesn't acknowledge the actual audience (trades operators) anywhere

## Audience Understanding

### Primary Audience: Trade and field service business leaders

**Profile**:
- Owner, operator, or operations leader at a trades business
- Industries: low voltage (security, fire, A/V), HVAC, plumbing, electrical/data, mechanical, multi-trade; often-good-fit adjacent areas include solar and refrigeration-only when the work pattern matches Simpro's core strengths
- Company size: typically 20–150 employees, $1M–$100M revenue (Simpro's sweet spot per FY26 ICP)
- Geography: **US-first** for simpro.com / simpro.ai (no AU/NZ/UK colloquialisms in US copy); AU, NZ, UK regional sites localize spelling and terms per `style-guide.md` regional table
- Buys software reluctantly, but values software that pays for itself within months
- Reads on phone between jobs; rarely reads marketing in a quiet office

### Customer Maturity Archetypes (Product Marketing Portal, February 2025)

Use these archetypes to shape the problem framing and proof, not as visible campaign labels unless a brief explicitly calls for them.

- **Empower — Business Seeking Modernization**: Established, often family-owned businesses moving from paper, spreadsheets, whiteboards, or disconnected basic tools. Lead with reduced admin, fewer data-entry errors, better office/field/customer communication, faster invoicing, and training/support that makes change feel manageable.
- **Elevate — Growth-Minded Expander**: Businesses that have already grown and need a more scalable field service management platform to expand into new locations, verticals, maintenance offers, or higher-margin work. Lead with scalable workflows, accounting and tech-stack integrations, reporting, profitability visibility, and growth without proportional overhead.
- **Excel — Mature Data-Driven Optimizer**: Larger or more advanced businesses with multiple divisions, locations, or entities. Lead with centralized operations, standardized workflows, cross-team coordination, compliance/safety consistency, resource allocation, and real-time reporting across jobs, inventory, productivity, profitability, and receivables.

### Best-Fit Targeting Boundaries (Product Marketing Portal, April 2025)

- **Best-fit environments**: residential, light commercial, and medium commercial work where the business manages small jobs, recurring contracts, maintenance, inspections, repairs, installations, and mid-sized projects.
- **Use caution in North America** when a prospect is mostly residential. Simpro can fit some residential businesses, but ServiceTitan, Jobber, Housecall Pro, FieldEdge, and Service Fusion are often stronger/lower-cost residential alternatives for smaller operators.
- **Limited fit**: heavy commercial work. Simpro may support some workflows, but businesses dominated by heavy commercial work often need to manage much of that work outside Simpro.
- **Not a fit / avoid leading with Simpro**: industrial workflows, general contractors, and complex projects that dominate the business. The framework notes industrial complexity can create 2x-4x implementation effort and mismatched expectations.
- **Qualification lens for content**: when writing for broad trade audiences, qualify the work mix by environment, work type, and complexity before assuming Simpro is the best answer. Strong Simpro-fit copy should emphasize small jobs, recurring contracts, maintenance, inspections, repairs, installations, and mid-sized projects.

### Secondary Audiences

- **Field technicians**: Care about the mobile experience, offline reliability, parts/inventory access, time to first paycheck.
- **Office staff / dispatchers / admins**: Care about scheduling, invoicing, reducing double-entry, accounting sync.
- **Finance / controller / CFO**: Care about job costing accuracy, cash flow, integration with QuickBooks/Xero, reporting.
- **Multi-location / private-equity-backed operators**: Care about roll-up reporting, multi-company architecture, standardization across acquired businesses.

### What They Care About

**Top Priorities**:
1. Margin per job — making more money on the work they already do
2. Faster cash — shrinking time from job done to invoice paid
3. Operational visibility — knowing what's happening across field and office in real time
4. Tech adoption that sticks — software the field will actually use
5. Scaling without chaos — growing the business without adding proportional admin headcount

**Pain Points**:
1. Job costing happens after the fact (or never) — they don't know which jobs make money
2. Double-entry between systems — same data typed into the FSM, the accounting tool, and a spreadsheet
3. Field techs hating the software and reverting to paper
4. Recurring maintenance falling through the cracks (asset compliance risks, missed revenue)
5. Quotes that take days to produce, losing jobs to faster competitors

### How to Serve Them

- **Respect their time**: Lead with the win. The "why this matters" goes in the headline, not the third paragraph.
- **Make it actionable**: Every piece of content ends with something the reader can do or evaluate.
- **Show the math**: Round numbers, named customers, specific outcomes — always.
- **Acknowledge the realities**: Adoption is hard. Onboarding takes weeks. Integrations break. Don't pretend otherwise.
- **Champion the trades**: Treat the industry as the backbone of the economy — because it is.

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing any content, verify:

- [ ] **Voice**: Sounds Simpro (authoritative, ambitious, trades-focused, practical, outcomes-driven)
- [ ] **Tone**: Appropriate for content type and audience stage
- [ ] **Specifics**: At least one named customer, named number, or named trade per major section
- [ ] **Clarity**: A trades operator on a job site phone could understand it
- [ ] **Accuracy**: Technical references (workflows, integrations, pricing logic) are correct
- [ ] **Action**: Reader has a clear next step
- [ ] **Messaging**: Aligns with current FY26 pillars (Profitability / AI-First / Operational Control / Trades Champion)
- [ ] **Terminology**: Uses trades vocabulary correctly
- [ ] **No filler**: Cut "very," "really," "actually," "robust," "seamless," "powerful" unless they earn their place
